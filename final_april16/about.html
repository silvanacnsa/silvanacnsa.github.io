Quebec's healthcare system issues and a rising trend of self-diagnosis

At the end of the year 2023, Montreal Gazette health reporter, Aaron Derfel, concluded that the Quebec healthcare system is “arguably the worst it's ever been”.

Learn More

A critical issue is a significant shortage of general practitioners, a problem exacerbated by physicians who retire, enter private practice, or leave the province altogether. In December 2023, around 14,000 patients had been waiting over a year for surgery (Derfel), and the previous year, over 800,000 Quebec residents were on the waitlist for a family physician (Ha).

There are many reasons why Quebec's healthcare system is in its current state. In 2019, Quebec experienced a 52 percent deficit in the number of physicians per capita compared to the average observed in other high-income countries with healthcare systems funded by universal taxpayer contributions (Shaw). Also, Quebec universities refuse more than 9,500 applicants per year to their medical faculties, capping the number of students admitted to 966 a year (Shaw). Additionally, for nearly 30 years, general practitioners have had to do compulsory shifts in hospitals and other facilities that are short-staffed, leaving a lack of physicians working in primary care (Ha). According to the Montreal Gazette, for Quebec's health network to resume functioning properly, there needs to be an increase of at least 12,425 more workers.

Self Diagnosis and Social Media

There is a concerning rising trend of young adults, relying on social media, and the Internet for self-diagnosing psychiatric disorders (Cohen). According to Johns Hopkins Medicine, mental health professionals are observing an increase in patients diagnosing themselves with various disorders or illnesses after watching social media creators share the symptoms of their health issues. This lack of professional guidance in these self-diagnosis can lead to misinformation and potentially harmful consequences, for example, an individual may conclude that they are suffering from depression, based on symptoms found online, but they could be experiencing hypothyroidism or a certain vitamin deficiency (Cohen).

This surge in self-diagnosis through internet sources may be partially attributed to the accessibility challenges of the healthcare system. With a growing number of individuals facing barriers to accessing timely and affordable healthcare, people are turning to the Internet for answers to their health concerns (Bregel). The limited availability of healthcare professionals, long wait times, and a shortage of accessible health services contribute to the appeal of online information.

Website Concept

In a time where securing a timely doctor's appointment can be a challenging task and waitlists seem endless, the website is a mock medical site intended to comment on the growing trend of self-diagnosis. This platform serves as a reference for those who don't have access to professional medical care and turn to the internet to get answers, offering lists of mundane symptoms and exaggerated and inaccurate diagnoses to hyperbolize the effect of getting medical advice online. We've all had an experience where we've googled a few of our symptoms and the results have ranged from a simple cold to an emergency situation.

The website addresses the serious issue of delayed medical attention and the resulting rise in self-diagnosis. By presenting absurd scenarios, the aim is to shed light on the challenges individuals face in accessing timely and accurate healthcare. The content within the website delves into the consequences of a healthcare system under strain, where individuals choose to seek information independently, even if it means entertaining inaccurate or exaggerated results.
